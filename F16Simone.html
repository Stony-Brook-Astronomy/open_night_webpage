Black Holes from Early Speculations to Modern Astrophysics

Dr. Simone Dall'Osso

Black holes are one of the most enigmatic and fascinating celestial bodies, that we can now observe—indirectly and directly—thanks to modern instruments able to reveal them both in high-energy radiation and even in gravitational wave emission. The history of the black hole idea can somehow be dated back to the physics of the XVIII century. However, a proper and meaningful understanding only became possible more than one century later, thanks to Einstein's General Theory of Relativity. Apparently, Einstein himself considered black holes as a mere mathematical oddity of his theory, with no real physical meaning. Although a black hole does not allow any type of radiation (nor information) to escape its interior (the so-called event horizon), its presence strongly affects matter and even the structure of space-time in its surrounding. It is these secondary effects that we can reveal and measure with our most advanced instruments. Thanks to the theoretical and observational efforts of the XX century, today we know that millions of black holes the size of our sun exist in our Galaxy, and in all galaxies, and that the center of most galaxies—including our own—harbor supermassive black holes millions to billion times larger in mass. Having revealed this astonishing picture is one of the great successes of modern astrophysics, and the future promises to be even more generous in insight and surprises.

Dr. Dall'Osso graduated in Astronomy at the University of Bologna (the oldest university in Europe founded over nine centuries ago, in the year 1088), and got his PhD in Astronomy at the University of Rome, specializing on the structure of ultramagnetic neutron stars (magnetars) and the mystery of their origin. From 2005 to 2010 he worked at the Astronomical Observatory of Rome and at Pisa University, further studying the physics of magnetars and their ability to emit X/gamma-rays and gravitational waves. He moved to the Hebrew University of Jerusalem from 2010 to 2013, where he started working on gamma-ray bursts, the most violent explosions in the universe, thought to harbour the formation of black holes in the collapse of massive stars.
